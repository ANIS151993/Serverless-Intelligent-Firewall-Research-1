Abstract

The proliferation of sophisticated network-based attacks demands intelligent, scalable, and cost-effective security solutions. This paper presents a Serverless Intelligent Firewall (SIF) architecture that integrates Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) deep learning with AWS Lambda serverless deployment and Zero-Trust principles to achieve real-time network intrusion detection. Evaluated on the CICIDS2017 benchmark dataset comprising 2.8 million labeled traffic flows across five attack categories (BENIGN, DoS, DDoS, PortScan, and Other), our LSTM model achieves 98% accuracy, 98% precision, 98% recall, and 98% F1-Score — outperforming CNN (93%), Decision Tree (90.2%), and SVM (88.4%) baselines. Statistical validation through paired t-tests (p<0.05) confirms the significance of performance gains. The proposed serverless deployment architecture demonstrates elastic scalability, sub-second inference latency, and significant cost reduction compared to traditional always-on firewall infrastructure, representing a novel convergence of deep learning, zero-trust security, and serverless computing paradigms.

Executive Summary

1.1 Overview

The contemporary cybersecurity landscape is defined by an escalating arms race between increasingly sophisticated adversarial attacks and the defensive infrastructure tasked with countering them. Traditional rule-based firewall systems — long the cornerstone of enterprise network security — are proving demonstrably insufficient against zero-day exploits, polymorphic malware, and adaptive distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) campaigns. Simultaneously, the migration of enterprise workloads towards cloud-native, serverless, and microservices architectures has fundamentally altered the threat surface, demanding a corresponding evolution in defensive posture.

This report presents a comprehensive research investigation into a novel Serverless Intelligent Firewall (SIF) framework that addresses these twin imperatives: intelligent, data-driven threat detection at scale, and deployment architectures that match the elasticity and cost-efficiency demands of modern cloud infrastructure. The research culminates in an IEEE 2025 publication authored by researchers at Gannon University and the University of the Potomac.

At the heart of the proposed system is a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent neural network trained on the CICIDS2017 benchmark dataset — a widely adopted reference dataset in network security research comprising over 2.8 million labeled network traffic flows. The LSTM model is chosen for its intrinsic capability to model temporal dependencies inherent in network packet sequences, making it uniquely suited to distinguishing benign traffic patterns from attack signatures that evolve across multiple time steps.

SECTION 2

Introduction and Motivation

2.1 Motivation

The global cybersecurity threat landscape has undergone a profound transformation in the past decade. The Internet of Things (IoT) expansion has connected billions of new devices to the internet, many with minimal built-in security. Cloud migration has moved critical organisational assets to shared, distributed infrastructure. Remote work has dissolved traditional network perimeters. Nation-state threat actors have elevated the sophistication of attack campaigns. And the commoditisation of attack toolkits through darknet marketplaces has lowered the barrier to entry for malicious actors of all capability levels.

According to Cybersecurity Ventures, the global cost of cybercrime reached approximately $8 trillion USD in 2023, with projections exceeding $10.5 trillion annually by 2025. Network-based attacks — including Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) campaigns, port scanning reconnaissance, and various forms of Denial of Service (DoS) exploitation — constitute a significant fraction of this threat landscape and represent vectors that an intelligent network firewall is uniquely positioned to address.

Conventional Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) and firewalls operate primarily on signature-based detection: comparing observed traffic against a curated database of known attack patterns. While effective against known, catalogued threats, signature-based systems are fundamentally reactive and demonstrate significant weaknesses against novel attack variants, zero-day vulnerabilities, and adversarially crafted traffic designed to evade pattern matching. The inability to generalise beyond the known threat signature space represents the core limitation of traditional approaches.

Anomaly-based detection systems — which establish a statistical baseline of normal behaviour and flag deviations — offer improved generalisation but historically suffer from high false positive rates that create operational burden and alarm fatigue. The challenge of balancing sensitivity (recall) against specificity (precision) in a high-throughput network environment remains a fundamental research problem. Machine learning, and particularly deep learning, offers a powerful framework for navigating this trade-off by learning rich, hierarchical representations of network traffic behaviour directly from data.

Concurrently, the rise of serverless computing — exemplified by platforms such as AWS Lambda, Google Cloud Functions, and Azure Functions — introduces a transformative operational paradigm for deploying computation workloads. Serverless architectures offer event-driven, on-demand execution with automatic scaling, granular billing, and elimination of infrastructure management overhead. These properties are highly attractive for security workloads, which are inherently event-driven (triggered by incoming traffic) and require rapid, elastic scaling to handle traffic volume fluctuations — including the very DDoS surges they are designed to detect.

Zero-Trust Architecture (ZTA), as formalised in NIST SP 800-207, establishes a security philosophy predicated on the principle that no network entity — whether inside or outside the traditional perimeter — should be trusted by default. Every access request must be continuously verified, authenticated, and authorised based on contextual signals. Implementing ZTA at the network layer requires precisely the kind of continuous, real-time traffic analysis that an intelligent firewall provides. The convergence of LSTM-based deep learning, serverless deployment, and zero-trust principles represents the core architectural contribution of this research.

2.2 Problem Statement

Despite significant advances in applying machine learning to network intrusion detection, several critical gaps persist in the current literature and practice:

Temporal Dependency Modelling: Network attacks frequently manifest as patterns that unfold over sequences of packets or flow windows, not as isolated events. Many ML approaches treat each network flow as an independent sample, discarding the temporal structure that carries important diagnostic information. Recurrent architectures such as LSTM are designed to capture precisely this kind of sequential dependency, yet their application in production-ready, deployable IDS systems remains underexplored.

Deployment Gap: A persistent challenge in applied machine learning is the gap between research prototype and production deployment. The majority of published intrusion detection research evaluates models in controlled, offline batch settings without addressing the engineering challenges of real-time inference at network speeds. Serverless deployment provides a compelling solution to this deployment gap, but integrating deep learning model serving within serverless constraints (memory limits, cold-start latency, statelessness) requires careful architectural design.

Scalability under Attack: Ironically, intrusion detection systems must be most robust precisely when they are under greatest load — during a DDoS attack. Traditional always-on server deployments may themselves become targets of resource exhaustion. A serverless IDS auto-scales with traffic volume, providing inherent resilience.

Zero-Trust Integration: Existing IDS solutions are rarely designed with explicit ZTA integration in mind, creating gaps in continuous verification coverage, particularly for east-west (internal network) traffic flows.

This research addresses each of these gaps through the design, implementation, and evaluation of the Serverless Intelligent Firewall framework.

SECTION 3

Background and Literature Review

This section situates the proposed Serverless Intelligent Firewall within the broader landscape of network security research, surveying foundational concepts and reviewing twelve closely related works. The review spans four thematic areas: classical intrusion detection systems, machine learning approaches for network security, zero-trust architectural frameworks, and serverless computing in security applications.

3.1 Network Intrusion Detection Systems: Foundations

Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS) have constituted a core layer of enterprise network security since the pioneering work of Denning [1] in 1987, which established the conceptual framework for statistical anomaly detection. The field has since bifurcated into two primary paradigms: signature-based detection, which compares observed traffic against a known-bad pattern library, and anomaly-based detection, which models normal behaviour statistically and flags deviations.

Snort [2] and Suricata represent the dominant open-source implementations of signature-based NIDS, relying on rule sets curated by security researchers. While highly accurate for known attack signatures and computationally efficient, these systems require continuous manual rule maintenance and cannot detect novel attack variants without prior rule creation. The Snort rule language, while powerful, imposes a significant ongoing operational burden and creates a temporal gap between attack discovery and defensive coverage.

The DARPA 1998/1999 datasets [3] were among the earliest large-scale benchmarks for IDS evaluation, though their synthetic nature and known biases eventually limited their representativeness. The KDD Cup 1999 dataset, derived from DARPA 1998 captures, became a de facto benchmark for ML-based IDS research through the 2000s, despite well-documented issues with feature redundancy and class imbalance. More recent benchmark datasets — including NSL-KDD (2009), UNSW-NB15 (2015), and CICIDS2017 [4] — address these limitations with more realistic traffic captures and broader attack variety.

The CICIDS2017 dataset, developed by the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity, represents the current gold standard for IDS evaluation research. Generated in a controlled network environment replicating realistic enterprise traffic, it includes Monday through Friday captures spanning normal traffic, web attacks, brute force, DoS, DDoS, infiltration, and botnet traffic. The dataset's 78 flow-level features, extracted using the CICFlowMeter tool, encompass statistical properties of both forward and backward packet streams, making it well-suited to machine learning classification tasks.

3.2 Machine Learning Approaches for Network Security

Classical Machine Learning: Support Vector Machines (SVM) were among the first ML methods applied systematically to network intrusion detection, leveraging their ability to define hyperplane decision boundaries in high-dimensional feature spaces. Mukherjee et al. [5] demonstrated that SVM classifiers could achieve competitive detection rates on KDD-derived benchmarks, though computational cost during training limits scalability to large datasets. Decision Trees offer interpretability advantages — classification rules can be directly inspected and validated by security analysts — and have been extensively applied to IDS tasks, typically achieving accuracy in the 85-92% range on modern datasets.

Random Forests and gradient boosting methods (XGBoost, LightGBM) have extended the ensemble paradigm with notable success, with several recent works reporting accuracies above 95% on CICIDS2017 subsets. However, these approaches share a fundamental limitation: they treat each network flow as an independent, identically distributed sample, ignoring the temporal structure of attack sequences.

Deep Learning Approaches: The application of deep neural networks to network security has accelerated substantially since 2017. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), originally developed for image recognition, have been adapted to network traffic classification by treating flow feature vectors as one-dimensional signals amenable to learned local feature extraction. CNNs demonstrate superior performance compared to classical methods on benchmark datasets, with Altunay et al. [6] reporting 93.21% accuracy on CICIDS2017 using a hybrid CNN+LSTM architecture in 2023.

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) and their variant Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are architecturally better suited to sequential data modelling than CNNs. LSTM networks, introduced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [7] in 1997, address the vanishing gradient problem of vanilla RNNs through gated memory cells that selectively retain and forget information over long sequences. In network security contexts, LSTM networks can model the temporal evolution of traffic flows, capturing attack signatures that span multiple time steps — a capability fundamentally absent in feed-forward and convolutional architectures.

Bamber et al. [8] (2025) applied a CNN-LSTM hybrid architecture to CICIDS2017 classification, achieving 95% accuracy and demonstrating that combining spatial feature extraction (CNN) with temporal sequence modelling (LSTM) yields synergistic improvements over either architecture alone. Their work provides a direct comparison point for the proposed SIF, which employs a pure LSTM architecture and outperforms their hybrid approach by 3 percentage points.

Federated and Privacy-Preserving Approaches: The FedSA framework [9] (2022) demonstrates the application of federated learning to intrusion detection, achieving 97% accuracy while preserving data locality — an important property for multi-organisation deployments where sharing raw network captures may violate privacy or regulatory constraints. The proposed SIF achieves superior accuracy (98%) in a centralised training setting, with federated extensions identified as future work.

Transformer-Based Approaches: The BERT and GPT-inspired attention mechanisms have been adapted for network traffic classification, with Transformer-IDS architectures appearing in recent literature. While showing promise, these approaches require substantially larger training datasets and computational budgets compared to LSTM networks, limiting their immediate practical applicability. Lin et al. [10] report Transformer-based IDS accuracy of 96.8% on CICIDS2017 but with a 4× increase in inference latency compared to LSTM baselines.

3.3 Zero-Trust Architecture Principles

The Zero-Trust Architecture (ZTA) paradigm, popularised by Forrester Research's John Kindervag around 2010 and formalised in NIST Special Publication 800-207 [11], represents a fundamental departure from perimeter-centric security models. The traditional "castle-and-moat" model implicitly trusted traffic originating from within the corporate network perimeter while scrutinising external traffic — a model rendered increasingly obsolete by cloud adoption, remote work, and supply chain attacks.

ZTA is predicated on three core tenets: (1) never trust, always verify — all entities must authenticate and be authorised regardless of network location; (2) assume breach — defensive architecture should assume that the network perimeter has been or will be compromised; (3) verify explicitly — access decisions should leverage all available data signals including identity, location, device health, service, workload, and data classification.

Rose et al. [11] elaborate seven ZTA design principles in NIST SP 800-207, with Principle 4 — "All communication is secured regardless of network location" — most directly motivating the SIF design. The SIF implements continuous traffic analysis consistent with ZTA requirements by evaluating every network flow against the trained LSTM classifier rather than relying on zone-based trust assignments.

Practical ZTA implementations require a Policy Decision Point (PDP) — the component responsible for evaluating access requests against policy — and a Policy Enforcement Point (PEP) — which grants or denies access based on PDP decisions. The SIF serves as an intelligent PDP, providing ML-derived risk assessments that augment or replace static rule-based policy evaluation. Ferraiolo et al. [12] discuss integration architectures for ML-based PDP components within established ZTA frameworks, identifying key interface points and trust requirements.

3.4 Serverless Computing in Security Contexts

Serverless computing — also known as Function-as-a-Service (FaaS) — enables developers to deploy discrete computational functions without managing underlying server infrastructure. AWS Lambda, Google Cloud Functions, and Azure Functions are the dominant commercial implementations. Serverless platforms provide automatic scaling from zero to thousands of concurrent invocations, sub-100ms billing granularity, and elimination of idle-resource costs.

The application of serverless computing to security workloads is relatively nascent in the literature. Datta et al. [13] demonstrate serverless deployment of intrusion detection models using lightweight ML classifiers (Random Forest, Naive Bayes) within AWS Lambda, achieving latencies of 150-400ms per inference — sufficient for asynchronous post-hoc analysis but challenging for inline blocking. The proposed SIF targets the same deployment platform but employs LSTM-based inference with Docker containerisation to support the larger model artefact size.

Kumar et al. [14] identify cold-start latency as the primary performance challenge for serverless ML inference, analysing warm/cold Lambda invocation patterns and recommending provisioned concurrency for latency-sensitive applications. The SIF architecture adopts this recommendation, using AWS Lambda provisioned concurrency for the inference function to maintain sub-100ms warm-start performance.

Container-based Lambda deployment (available since AWS Lambda Extensions in 2020) enables packaging of models exceeding the 250MB direct upload limit, supporting deployment of the SIF's TensorFlow/Keras model artefact within a Docker image up to 10GB. This architectural enabler is fundamental to the feasibility of LSTM deployment within the Lambda environment.

3.5 Research Gaps and Opportunities

Synthesising the literature review, three primary research gaps motivate the proposed SIF:

Research Gap 1: Temporal Modelling in Deployable IDS Existing deployable IDS solutions predominantly use stateless, flow-level classifiers that discard temporal structure. LSTM networks offer superior temporal modelling but have not been integrated with production-ready serverless deployment architectures.
Research Gap 2: Serverless Deep Learning IDS Published serverless IDS work is limited to lightweight classical ML models. Deploying deep learning models (LSTM, CNN) within serverless constraints requires novel engineering solutions including Docker containerisation and provisioned concurrency management.
Research Gap 3: ZTA-Aligned Intelligent Firewall Design No existing published work explicitly designs an ML-based IDS with formal ZTA alignment, integrating with PDP/PEP components and providing continuous verification semantics consistent with NIST SP 800-207.

SECTION 4

Dataset Analysis — CICIDS2017

4.1 Dataset Overview and Provenance

The Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity Intrusion Detection Dataset 2017 (CICIDS2017) is a widely adopted benchmark in network security research, designed to address the limitations of earlier datasets through realistic traffic generation, comprehensive labelling, and a broad spectrum of attack categories. The dataset was generated at the University of New Brunswick's network facility over a one-week period (Monday through Friday), replicating enterprise network activity using traffic profiles derived from real network behavioural analysis.

Traffic was captured using the CICFlowMeter network flow generator and packet analyser, which aggregates raw packet captures (PCAPs) into flow-level records. A network flow is defined as a uni-directional sequence of packets sharing the same source IP, destination IP, source port, destination port, and protocol, within a defined timeout window. Each flow is characterised by 78 numerical features computed from packet inter-arrival times, packet lengths, flag counts, and flow duration statistics.

The dataset encompasses benign traffic generated by 25 users executing seven protocols (HTTP, HTTPS, FTP, SSH, and email) alongside deliberate attack injection across five categories. The resulting dataset totals approximately 2.8 million labelled flow records — a scale sufficient to train and evaluate deep learning models without underfitting concerns.

Feature selection was performed using a combination of variance thresholding (removing zero-variance features such as Bwd PSH Flags and Fwd URG Flags on certain protocol subsets) and correlation analysis to identify and remove highly correlated feature pairs (Pearson |r| > 0.95). After this process, all 78 features were retained for model training, as preliminary ablation studies showed that removing any feature group reduced LSTM validation accuracy by 0.5–2.3%, suggesting that each category contributes unique discriminative information.

Features exhibiting infinite values (arising from division-by-zero in rate computations for flows of zero duration) were identified and replaced using the median imputation strategy. Negative flow duration values — an artefact of timestamp synchronisation in the original captures — were removed as invalid records. Stage 1: Data Ingestion and Normalisation

All five CICIDS2017 daily CSV files are loaded and concatenated. Column names contain trailing whitespace in the original files; a strip operation is applied to all column identifiers. The target column "Label" is mapped to a standardised five-class taxonomy: BENIGN → 0, DoS → 1, DDoS → 2, PortScan → 3, Other → 4, where "Other" encompasses Brute Force, Web Attacks, Infiltration, Bot, and Heartbleed records.

Stage 2: Data Cleaning

Records containing infinite values in any feature column are removed (numpy.isinf detection across the feature matrix). Records with NaN values are similarly dropped. Duplicate rows — defined as exact matches across all 78 features plus the label — are removed. These operations reduce the dataset from approximately 2,832,144 raw records to 2,830,743 clean records (loss of <0.1%).

Stage 3: Feature Scaling

All 78 numerical features are normalised to the [0, 1] range using Min-Max scaling computed on the training partition exclusively. The scaling parameters (minimum and maximum per feature) are fitted on training data only and applied identically to validation and test partitions, preventing data leakage. Min-Max normalisation is preferred over standardisation for LSTM inputs as it preserves zero-valued features (present in sparse flag counts) and ensures bounded gradient magnitudes during back-propagation through time (BPTT).

The Min-Max normalisation formula for feature j:

x'ij = (xij − minj) / (maxj − minj) (Eq. 1) Min-Max Feature Normalisation

Stage 4: Class Imbalance Handling

To address the severe class imbalance described in Section 4.2, class weights are computed inversely proportional to class frequency and applied as sample weights during LSTM training. The class weight formula is:

wc = Ntotal / (k × Nc) (Eq. 2) Class Weight Computation (N=total samples, k=number of classes, N_c=samples in class c)

Stage 5: Sequence Reshaping for LSTM

LSTM networks require 3D input tensors of shape (samples, time_steps, features). Since individual CICIDS2017 records are single flow snapshots rather than raw packet sequences, we adopt a sliding window approach to construct synthetic sequences. Each training sample is formed as a window of T consecutive flow records (T=10 in our configuration), creating a temporal context that allows the LSTM to model network behaviour evolution. The reshaping transforms the 2D feature matrix [N, 78] into a 3D tensor [N−T+1, T, 78].

5.2 LSTM Mathematical Formulation

The LSTM cell is the fundamental computational unit of the model. Each LSTM cell maintains a hidden state ht and a cell state ct, modulated by three gating mechanisms. The complete LSTM gate equations at time step t are:

Forget Gate:   ft = σ(Wf · [ht−1, xt] + bf) (Eq. 3) Controls what information from the previous cell state is discarded
Input Gate:   it = σ(Wi · [ht−1, xt] + bi),   gt = tanh(Wg · [ht−1, xt] + bg) (Eq. 4) Controls what new information is written to the cell state
Cell State Update:   ct = ft ⊙ ct−1 + it ⊙ gt (Eq. 5) Blends retained and new information to form the updated cell state
Output Gate:   ot = σ(Wo · [ht−1, xt] + bo),   ht = ot ⊙ tanh(ct) (Eq. 6) Controls what information the hidden state exposes to subsequent layers

Where σ denotes the sigmoid activation function, tanh the hyperbolic tangent, and ⊙ the Hadamard (element-wise) product. Wf, Wi, Wg, Wo are learned weight matrices, and bf, bi, bg, bo are bias vectors. The parameter count for a single LSTM layer with input dimension d and hidden dimension h is: 4 × (d + h) × h + 4h.

The output of the final LSTM layer passes through a dense (fully connected) layer with softmax activation to produce a probability distribution over the five traffic classes:

ŷc = softmax(Wdense · hT + bdense)c = exp(zc) / Σk exp(zk) (Eq. 7) Softmax output layer producing per-class probability estimates

The model is trained to minimise the categorical cross-entropy loss, weighted by class weights wc to address class imbalance:

L = −(1/N) Σn Σc wc · ync · log(ŷnc) (Eq. 8) Weighted categorical cross-entropy loss function

5.3 Model Architecture Design

Figure 1. Proposed three-layer LSTM model architecture. Hidden units decrease across layers (128→64→32) with dropout regularisation (p=0.3) applied after each LSTM layer. A dense softmax output layer produces five-class probability estimates.

The proposed LSTM model comprises three stacked LSTM layers with decreasing hidden unit counts, followed by dropout regularisation and a dense output layer. The architectural decisions are motivated by the following considerations:

Layer Depth (3 layers): Three stacked LSTM layers provide sufficient representational capacity to model complex, multi-scale temporal dependencies in network traffic while remaining computationally tractable for serverless deployment. Ablation experiments showed that adding a fourth layer provided less than 0.2% accuracy improvement with a 35% increase in parameter count and inference latency.

Hidden Unit Reduction (128→64→32): The decreasing hidden unit sequence implements a learned bottleneck, progressively compressing the temporal representation toward a compact feature vector suitable for classification. This hierarchical compression encourages the model to extract increasingly abstract representations at each layer.

Dropout Regularisation (p=0.3): Dropout with probability 0.3 is applied after each LSTM layer during training, randomly zeroing 30% of hidden unit activations per forward pass. This prevents co-adaptation of hidden units and substantially reduces overfitting, as evidenced by the close alignment of training and validation accuracy curves (see Section 7.2).

11.1 Paired t-Test Methodology

To confirm that the LSTM model's performance improvements over baselines are statistically significant rather than attributable to random variation, paired t-tests were conducted on per-sample classification outcomes. The paired design is appropriate here because each model is evaluated on identical test samples, enabling direct pairwise comparison of prediction correctness.

For each pair of models (LSTM vs. baseline), a binary vector of per-sample correctness is constructed: di = 1 if the model correctly classifies sample i, 0 otherwise. The paired t-test evaluates whether the mean difference d̄ = mean(dLSTM − dbaseline) is statistically distinguishable from zero. The test statistic is:

t = d̄ / (sd / √n) (Eq. 9) Paired t-test statistic (s_d = standard deviation of differences, n = number of test samples)

With n = 424,611 test samples, the test has extremely high statistical power, enabling detection of even small practical differences. All tests use a two-tailed design with significance threshold α = 0.05. Bonferroni correction is applied for multiple comparisons (three comparisons: LSTM vs. CNN, LSTM vs. DT, LSTM vs. SVM), yielding a corrected significance threshold of α' = 0.05/3 ≈ 0.0167.

SECTION 12

Discussion and Implications

12.1 Interpretation of Results

The experimental results establish several important insights about the application of deep learning to network intrusion detection and the viability of serverless deployment architectures for intelligent security systems.

LSTM Superiority Explained: The LSTM model's 5-percentage-point advantage over the CNN baseline cannot be attributed to parameter count alone (LSTM: 168,997 parameters vs. CNN: ~145,000 parameters). Rather, the performance gap is fundamentally architectural: the LSTM's gated memory mechanism enables it to model long-range temporal dependencies across a 10-step flow sequence, while the CNN's local receptive field is limited to 3-step convolutional windows. Network attacks — particularly DoS and DDoS — manifest as sustained temporal patterns rather than instantaneous events, and the LSTM's recurrent processing is specifically designed to encode such patterns.

The near-identical performance across accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-Score (all at 98%) is particularly notable. This balance indicates that the class weighting strategy effectively compensated for dataset imbalance, producing a model that maintains high sensitivity to minority attack classes (recall) without sacrificing specificity for the majority benign class (precision).

Serverless Deployment Viability: The successful containerised deployment of the LSTM inference function within AWS Lambda represents a proof-of-concept demonstration that production-quality deep learning inference is feasible within serverless constraints. The key enabling factor is Docker container packaging, which eliminates the 250MB Lambda layer size restriction and enables deployment of the TensorFlow/Keras runtime alongside the model artefact within a 10GB container image budget. Measured warm-start inference latency of approximately 45ms per batch of 64 flows is compatible with asynchronous traffic analysis workflows, enabling the SIF to process flow records generated by enterprise networks at realistic volumes.

Zero-Trust Integration: The SIF's integration with AWS IAM for function execution permissions, VPC endpoint policies for API Gateway access, and CloudTrail audit logging for all inference requests collectively implements the ZTA tenet of "verify explicitly." Every inference request is authenticated (IAM), authorised (resource-based policies), and logged (CloudTrail), providing the continuous verification audit trail required by NIST SP 800-207 Section 2.2.

12.2 Practical Implications for Network Security Operations

Cost Implications: Serverless billing granularity (per 1ms of execution time) fundamentally alters the economics of continuous network monitoring. A traditional always-on IDS server running at $0.10/hour (t3.medium equivalent) costs approximately $876/year regardless of traffic volume. The SIF Lambda function, processing 100,000 network flows per hour at 45ms per batch of 64 flows, incurs approximately 70 Lambda invocations/minute. At $0.0000000083 per ms-GB (AWS Lambda pricing), this corresponds to approximately $2.40/day or $876/year — comparable to always-on at high utilisation but dramatically cheaper at lower traffic volumes (Lambda scales to zero cost during off-hours).

Operational Integration: The SIF's JSON API interface enables straightforward integration with existing SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) platforms via AWS EventBridge rules or SNS topic subscriptions. Security Operations Centre (SOC) analysts can receive real-time alerts for high-confidence attack classifications (LSTM confidence score > 0.95) while lower-confidence classifications are flagged for manual review. This tiered alert design balances automation with human oversight — a critical requirement for operational security systems.

Regulatory Compliance: The serverless architecture provides inherent compliance advantages. AWS Lambda functions execute within isolated execution environments with no persistent state, reducing the attack surface compared to long-running server processes. CloudTrail audit logs provide tamper-evident records of all model inferences, supporting compliance requirements under frameworks including NIST Cybersecurity Framework, SOC 2 Type II, and ISO 27001.

12.3 Zero-Trust Architecture Implications

The proposed SIF represents a practical realisation of ZTA Principle 2 — "All traffic is treated as potentially hostile until proven otherwise" — at the network layer. By subjecting every observed flow to LSTM classification rather than relying on network-zone trust assumptions, the SIF implements continuous verification that extends ZTA principles to the packet-level. This is particularly valuable in modern hybrid cloud environments where the traditional network perimeter is dissolving.

The integration of per-flow ML scoring with dynamic Security Group updates enables adaptive microsegmentation — a ZTA capability that traditional firewalls cannot provide. When the LSTM classifies a flow as high-confidence DDoS (confidence > 0.99), the SIF can automatically update VPC Security Group rules to block the source IP for a configurable quarantine period, implementing automated threat response consistent with ZTA's "assume breach" principle.

Practitioner Recommendation Organisations seeking to implement the SIF in production environments should begin with asynchronous monitoring mode (SIF operates on flow logs, not inline) to build confidence in false positive rates before transitioning to inline blocking mode. The recommended operational threshold for automated blocking is LSTM confidence score > 0.98, which reduces false-positive blocking incidents to approximately 0.1% of benign flows.
SECTION 13

Limitations and Future Work

13.1 Current Limitations

Dataset Currency and Adversarial Robustness: CICIDS2017, while widely adopted, was generated in 2017 and does not capture attack patterns from the past seven years, including AI-generated polymorphic attacks, supply chain compromises, or novel ransomware propagation techniques. Models trained on this dataset may not generalise to more recent attack families without periodic retraining on updated traffic captures. Additionally, the dataset was generated in a controlled lab environment; real-world traffic exhibits greater variability and noise than the controlled capture conditions.

Cold-Start Latency: AWS Lambda functions experience cold-start latency (200-800ms) when invoked after an idle period. While provisioned concurrency mitigates this for predictable traffic patterns, unexpected traffic surges may trigger cold-start delays that affect the timeliness of threat detection. This is not a limitation for asynchronous analysis workflows but represents a constraint for inline blocking applications.

Single-Dataset Evaluation: All experimental results are reported on CICIDS2017 exclusively. Cross-dataset generalisation — the ability of the trained model to classify flows from different network environments — has not been evaluated. Network traffic characteristics (protocol distributions, flow duration statistics, background traffic mix) vary substantially between organisations, and models trained on CICIDS2017 may require domain adaptation for deployment in significantly different network environments.

CPU-Only Inference: The experimental setup and Lambda deployment use CPU-only TensorFlow for LSTM inference, as Lambda functions do not currently support GPU execution. While the LSTM architecture (168,997 parameters) is sufficiently compact for efficient CPU inference, higher-throughput deployments might benefit from GPU-accelerated inference via Amazon SageMaker endpoints or EC2 GPU instances for batch processing contexts.

13.2 Future Research Directions

Federated Learning Extension: Extending the SIF to federated training would enable multi-organisation model training across distributed network environments without sharing raw traffic data — a critical privacy and regulatory consideration. Federated LSTM training (inspired by FedSA [9]) would potentially improve model generalisation by exposing it to diverse network traffic patterns while preserving data locality.

Adversarial Robustness: Evaluating and improving the SIF's robustness to adversarially crafted traffic — network flows designed to evade ML-based detection — is an important research priority. Adversarial training techniques (adding adversarial examples to the training set) and certified robustness approaches (interval bound propagation for LSTM networks) should be explored.

Explainability Integration: Incorporating LSTM attention mechanisms and post-hoc explainability methods (LIME, SHAP) would enable the SIF to provide human-interpretable rationale for its classifications, supporting analyst review and model validation in regulated environments.

Real-Time Packet-Level Processing: The current SIF operates on pre-computed flow-level features, requiring a CICFlowMeter-equivalent preprocessing step before LSTM inference. Future work should explore architectures capable of classifying raw packet sequences in real-time, eliminating the pre-processing latency and enabling inline blocking at line rate.

Multi-Cloud and Hybrid Deployments: Extending the SIF architecture to multi-cloud environments (AWS + Azure + GCP) and hybrid on-premises/cloud deployments would increase its applicability to enterprise environments with complex, heterogeneous infrastructure.

SECTION 14

Conclusion

This paper has presented the Serverless Intelligent Firewall (SIF), a novel framework that unifies three cutting-edge paradigms — LSTM-based deep learning for temporal traffic analysis, AWS Lambda serverless deployment for elastic and cost-efficient operation, and Zero-Trust Architecture principles for principled security posture — into a cohesive and production-viable network intrusion detection solution.

Evaluated comprehensively on the CICIDS2017 benchmark dataset comprising 2.8 million labeled network flow records across five traffic classes, the proposed three-layer LSTM model achieves state-of-the-art performance: 98% accuracy, 98% precision, 98% recall, and 98% F1-Score. These results are statistically confirmed as significantly superior to three established baseline classifiers (CNN, Decision Tree, SVM) through paired t-tests with Bonferroni correction (p < 0.001), and represent improvements of 5, 7.8, and 9.6 percentage points over CNN, Decision Tree, and SVM respectively.

Class-wise analysis demonstrates near-perfect detection of the highest-impact attack categories: DDoS (recall=1.00) and PortScan (recall=1.00) are detected with essentially no missed attacks, while DoS achieves 99% recall. The LSTM's advantage over the CNN baseline is most pronounced for attack classes with complex temporal evolution, validating the architectural hypothesis that recurrent networks with gated memory are intrinsically better suited to network traffic classification than stateless alternatives.

The serverless deployment architecture, leveraging Docker containerisation for Lambda function packaging, demonstrates the feasibility of deploying production-quality deep learning inference within managed serverless infrastructure. This deployment model eliminates operational overhead, provides automatic scaling from zero to thousands of concurrent invocations, and aligns costs with actual traffic volume — making enterprise-grade intelligent network monitoring economically accessible for organisations of all sizes.

The explicit alignment of the SIF with NIST SP 800-207 Zero-Trust Architecture principles — through continuous per-flow verification, dynamic adaptive microsegmentation, and comprehensive audit logging — positions the framework as a practical realisation of ZTA's network-layer requirements. As organisations continue migrating toward cloud-native, perimeter-less architectures, the demand for intelligent, continuously-verifying network security capabilities will only intensify.

The research contributions of this paper — SOTA LSTM classification performance, serverless deployment architecture, ZTA integration framework, and comprehensive comparative evaluation — collectively represent a meaningful advance toward the vision of intelligent, adaptive, and economically sustainable network security infrastructure. Future work will extend these results through federated learning for multi-organisation privacy-preserving training, adversarial robustness evaluation, and real-time packet-level processing architectures.

A.3 Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)

The LSTM is trained via gradient-based optimisation using Adam, which requires computing gradients of the loss L with respect to all trainable parameters. The BPTT algorithm unrolls the LSTM over T time steps and applies the chain rule. Key gradient expressions:

∂L/∂ht = ∂L/∂ht(direct) + ∂L/∂ht+1 · ∂ht+1/∂ht (A.7) Hidden state gradient includes both direct output gradient and future gradient flowing back through time
∂L/∂ct = ∂L/∂ht ⊙ ot ⊙ (1 − tanh²(ct)) + ∂L/∂ct+1 ⊙ ft+1 (A.8) Cell state gradient — the additive structure prevents vanishing gradients over long sequences

The additive update structure of Equation A.5 (ct = ft ⊙ ct-1 + it ⊙ gt) is the fundamental property that distinguishes LSTM from vanilla RNN and enables learning of long-range dependencies. The gradient ∂L/∂ct flows back through the addition operation without multiplicative attenuation, preventing the vanishing gradient problem that afflicts vanilla RNNs for sequences longer than approximately 10 steps.
